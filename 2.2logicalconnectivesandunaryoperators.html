Logical Connectives and Unary Operators

Logic is the framework in which is one able to understand how to move from one result to the next. Without logic, no reasonable argument or proof could be constructed. It is therefore a crucial concept to understand how one connects different propositional statements using logic. In logic, the process of connecting two statements is called a logical connective and introduced in the subsequent definition.

Definition 2.1: Logical Connectives/Operators

A logical connective/operator is an operator that takes one or two propositional variables and produces a new propositional variable.

The logical operators that take one propositional variable and produce a new propositional variable are called unary operators whilst logical operators that take two propositional variables and produce a new propositional variable are called binary operators . Due to the restricted number of outputs a propositional variable can take there are few distinct unary operators. In fact, it is the case that there are only four possible unary operators. These operators are the negation , identity , tautology and contradiction operators denoted by $ \neg $, $ \text{id} $, $ \top $ and $ \bot $ respectively. The precise definitions of these operators will be introduced later along with some useful binary operators.

Definition 2.2: Compound Proposition

A compound proposition is a propositional variable that contains logical connectives.

The propositional variables that contain no logical connectives are called atomic propositions . In other words, the atomic propositions are the propositional variables that cannot be divided into further smaller propositional variables.

Definition 2.3: Valuation

A valuation is an assignment of all the possible values a compound proposition can take given all the possible values of its component atomic propositions.

A particularly useful way of representing valuations is through a truth table. A truth table is constructed by exploring all the possible truth values of the atomic propositions that build up the compound proposition, and then assigning the corresponding truth values of the compound proposition. Truth tables can be used to define logical connectives as well as proving equivalences of two different propositional variables.

Definition 2.4: Unary Operators

The unary operators $ \neg $, $ \text{id} $, $ \top $ and $ \bot $ are defined via the following truth table: \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c|c|c} P & \neg P & \text{id}(P) & \top P & \bot P\\ \hline T & F & T & T & F\\ F & T & F & T & F \end{array} \end{equation*}

Exploring each of the unary operators individually it becomes clear where their definitions emerge from. The negation operator $ \neg $ simply negates the truth value of the propositional variable. That is, if a proposition is true its negation is false and vice-versa. The identity operator $ \text{id} $ doesn't change the truth value of the propositional variable. It acts exactly the same way as to a number being multiplied by one. The names of the final two unary operators become immediately apparent from the following two definitions.

Definition 2.5: Tautology

A tautology is a propositional variable whose valuation is always true.

Definition 2.6: Contradiction

A contradiction is a propositional variable whose valuation is always false.

That is, the tautology operator $ \top $ assigns all values to be true and the contradiction operator $ \bot $ assigns all values to be false.
